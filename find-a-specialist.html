How to Find a Veterinary Specialist — When You Need One and How to Get There

Your general practice vet is excellent — but some conditions require the depth of training that only a board-certified specialist can bring. Here's how to know when it's time, and exactly how to navigate the referral process.

👩‍⚕️
Dr. Amanda Reyes, DVM, DACVIM
Diplomate, American College of Veterinary Internal Medicine
Updated May 2025 📖 9 min read

Veterinary specialization has transformed what's possible in pet medicine. Thirty years ago, a dog with a brain tumor had no meaningful treatment options. Today, veterinary neurologists perform MRI-guided brain surgery with outcomes that rival human neurosurgery. A cat with congestive heart failure can be managed by a veterinary cardiologist using the same diagnostic tools — echocardiography, electrocardiography, interventional catheterization — as a human cardiologist.

But accessing this level of care requires knowing it exists, knowing when your pet needs it, and knowing how to get there. Most pet owners only find out about veterinary specialists after a general practitioner refers them — often later than ideal.

The Key Principle

You don't need a referral to see a veterinary specialist at most specialty practices — you can self-refer. A referral from your GP vet is helpful and provides valuable records, but it's not a prerequisite. If you believe your pet needs specialist care, you can call a specialty practice directly.

12 Situations That Warrant a Specialist

These are the clinical situations where specialist involvement consistently improves outcomes:

  • Diagnosis unclear after initial workup — if your vet has run basic diagnostics and the picture is still unclear, an internist can design and interpret advanced diagnostic protocols
  • Condition not responding to standard treatment — a specialist sees hundreds of cases of a specific condition yearly; a GP may see a handful
  • Cancer diagnosis or suspicion — an oncologist will know about clinical trials, targeted therapies, and protocols your GP may not
  • Heart murmur or cardiac abnormality — echocardiography and interpretation requires specialized training
  • Neurological symptoms — seizures, sudden paralysis, vestibular episodes, head tilt, circling all warrant neurological evaluation
  • Eye conditions beyond surface level — cataracts, glaucoma, retinal disease, and eye injuries benefit from ophthalmological expertise
  • Surgery beyond general practice scope — joint replacement, TPLO, spinal surgery, abdominal mass removal
  • Kidney disease management — an internist with nephrology focus will optimize protocols for CKD stages 3 and 4
  • Endocrine disorders — Cushing's, Addison's, diabetes with complications, and thyroid disease management
  • Respiratory disease — chronic coughing, collapsing trachea, laryngeal paralysis
  • Reproductive issues — pyometra complications, breeding management, pregnancy complications
  • Second opinion on a major diagnosis — always appropriate before committing to an expensive or irreversible treatment path

The Major Veterinary Specialties

Board certification in veterinary medicine requires completing an accredited residency (typically 3 years post-DVM) and passing rigorous national board examinations. The diplomate designation (e.g., DACVIM) indicates board certification in that specialty.

🫀
Veterinary Internal Medicine
DACVIM (Internal Medicine)
The broadest specialist category — internal medicine specialists handle complex multi-system disease, chronic illness, and difficult diagnostic cases. Many subspecialize further in cardiology, neurology, or oncology.
Kidney Disease Diabetes Liver Disease GI Disorders Immune Disorders Respiratory Disease
🧠
Veterinary Neurology
DACVIM (Neurology)
Neurologists diagnose and treat disorders of the brain, spinal cord, and peripheral nervous system. They perform MRI and CT interpretation, CSF analysis, and spinal surgeries.
Seizures IVDD Brain Tumors Vestibular Disease Paralysis
❤️
Veterinary Cardiology
DACVIM (Cardiology)
Cardiologists diagnose and manage heart disease using echocardiography, electrocardiography, Holter monitoring, and interventional procedures including balloon valvuloplasty and pacemaker implantation.
Heart Murmurs DCM Arrhythmias Heart Failure Congenital Defects
🎗️
Veterinary Oncology
DACVIM (Oncology) or DACVR (Radiation Oncology)
Oncologists manage cancer diagnosis, staging, chemotherapy, immunotherapy, and coordinate with radiation oncologists for radiation therapy. They also have access to clinical trials.
Lymphoma Mast Cell Tumors Osteosarcoma Hemangiosarcoma Transitional Cell Carcinoma
👁️
Veterinary Ophthalmology
DACVO
Ophthalmologists diagnose and treat eye conditions using specialized equipment not available in general practice, including slit lamps, tonometers, electroretinography, and perform cataract surgery and other ocular procedures.
Cataracts Glaucoma PRA Corneal Ulcers Retinal Disease
🦴
Veterinary Surgery
DACVS
Board-certified surgeons perform complex orthopedic, soft tissue, and neurosurgical procedures requiring advanced training beyond general practice. TPLO, hip replacement, spinal surgery, and complex tumor removal are common procedures.
TPLO / CCL Repair Hip Replacement Spinal Surgery Mass Removal Reconstructive Surgery

How to Get a Referral — Step by Step

1
Talk to Your GP Vet First
Tell your vet you'd like a specialist referral. Most GPs welcome this — it demonstrates you're engaged in your pet's care and relieves them of managing a case outside their expertise. A good vet will never be offended by a referral request.
2
Collect All Records Before Your Appointment
Request complete records from your GP: all blood work, urinalysis, imaging (X-rays, ultrasound), pathology reports, and a list of all medications trialed. The more complete the record, the less duplicate testing the specialist needs to order.
3
Find the Right Specialist
Use the Vets.co specialist directory, the American College of Veterinary Internal Medicine (ACVIM) referral directory, or ask your GP for a recommendation. Verify board certification — the DACVIM or equivalent designation confirms residency training and board exam passage.
4
Prepare Questions Before the Appointment
Write down: the timeline of symptoms, any treatments tried and their response, your observations at home, and your specific goals and concerns. Specialist appointments are more productive when the owner is prepared.
5
Maintain Communication with Your GP
Request that the specialist send a full consultation report to your GP vet. Continuity between your primary care vet and specialist is essential for ongoing management, and your GP will continue to handle routine care.
